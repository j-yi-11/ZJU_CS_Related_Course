<span class="abstract-full has-text-grey-dark mathjax" id="2311.10054v1-abstract-full" style="display: none;">
Prompting serves as the major way humans interact with Large <span class="search-hit mathjax">Language</span> <span class="search-hit mathjax">Models</span> (LLM). Commercial AI systems commonly define the role of the LLM in system prompts. For example, ChatGPT uses &#34;You are a helpful assistant&#34; as part of the default system prompt. But is &#34;a helpful assistant&#34; the best role for LLMs? In this study, we present a systematic <span class="search-hit mathjax">evaluation</span> of how social roles in system prompts affect <span class="search-hit mathjax">model</span> performance. We curate a list of 162 roles covering 6 types of interpersonal relationships and 8 types of occupations. Through extensive analysis of 3 popular LLMs and 2457 questions, we show that adding interpersonal roles in prompts consistently improves the <span class="search-hit mathjax">models</span>&#39; performance over a range of questions. Moreover, while we find that using gender-neutral roles and specifying the role as the audience leads to better performances, predicting which role leads to the best performance remains a challenging task, and that frequency, similarity, and perplexity do not fully explain the effect of social roles on <span class="search-hit mathjax">model</span> performances. Our results can help inform the design of system prompts for AI systems. Code and data are available at https://github.com/Jiaxin-Pei/Prompting-with-Social-Roles.

<span class="abstract-full has-text-grey-dark mathjax" id="2311.09993v1-abstract-full" style="display: none;">
Automatic hate speech detection using deep neural <span class="search-hit mathjax">models</span> is hampered by the scarcity of labeled datasets, leading to poor generalization. To mitigate this problem, generative AI has been utilized to generate large amounts of synthetic hate speech sequences from available labeled examples, leveraging the generated data in finetuning large pre-trained <span class="search-hit mathjax">language</span> <span class="search-hit mathjax">models</span> (LLMs). Hallucinations are concerning because they erode the reliability of LLMs and raise safety issues. Pruning is a technique that reduces <span class="search-hit mathjax">model</span> size by removing redundant weights to create sparse <span class="search-hit mathjax">models</span> that enable more efficient inference. Pruned <span class="search-hit mathjax">models</span> yield comparable performance to their counterpart full-sized <span class="search-hit mathjax">models</span>, making them ideal alternatives when operating on a limited budget. In this paper, we provide an extensive empirical study on the hallucinations produced by pruned <span class="search-hit mathjax">models</span> across three standard summarization tasks, two pruning approaches, three instruction-tuned LLMs, and three hallucination <span class="search-hit mathjax">evaluation</span> metrics. Our follow-up analysis suggests that pruned <span class="search-hit mathjax">models</span> tend to depend more on the source input and less on their parametric knowledge from pre-training for generation. This greater dependency on the source input leads to a higher lexical overlap between generated content and the source input, which can be a reason for the reduction in hallucinations.
